# Campaign Strategy Notes

## Election context

- November 3, 2026 general election, midterm year
- First Multnomah County election using ranked choice voting
- No primary. All candidates go straight to the November ballot
- Filing deadline: August 25, 2026 (also voters' pamphlet statement deadline)
- Ballots mailed: October 14, 2026
- Open seat: Singleton is running for County Chair
- Nonpartisan race

## Vote math

- Singleton won District 2 in November 2024 (presidential year) with ~55,400 votes at 72% turnout
- Jayapal won District 2 in May 2022 (midterm primary) with ~24,500 votes
- Midterm November turnout is typically 55-65%, lower than presidential years
- Realistic winning number: 20,000-35,000 votes depending on field size and RCV dynamics

## RCV strategy

- Single seat: winner must reach a majority through elimination rounds
- Need enough first-choice votes to survive early rounds
- Need to avoid being polarizing so eliminated candidates' voters rank you second or third
- First-choice base comes from personal contact, endorsements, and the voters' pamphlet
- Second and third-choice rankings come from name recognition and a non-threatening, credible presence
- "Be everyone's acceptable second choice" is right, but not sufficient on its own. Needs a solid first-choice floor.

## Voter channels (ranked by impact)

### Voters' pamphlet
Single highest-impact piece. Every registered voter receives one. For a low-attention county commissioner race, many voters will decide entirely from the 325-word statement and listed endorsements. This is the most important piece of writing the campaign produces.

### Endorsements
Each endorsement activates an existing network. Union endorsements (AFSCME, SEIU, etc.) come with member voter guides and direct communications. Political endorsements (Tom Potter confirmed) signal credibility to specific voter segments. Each endorsing organization does outreach to its own members at no cost to the campaign. Singleton won with less money than Adams by having a wider endorsement network.

### Door knocking and palm cards
Most effective per-contact channel. Research shows door-to-door canvassing raises turnout by about 6%. At 40-50 doors per hour, 1,000 doors is 20-25 hours of walking. Contact rate is ~25-30%, so 1,000 doors produces 250-300 conversations. Of those, roughly 25-45 become first-choice voters, 75-120 rank Nabil somewhere on the ballot, and pass-along to household members adds another 80-100 indirect touches. Early canvassing (summer) is best for persuasion, late canvassing (October) is best for turnout. Volunteers with palm cards at farmers markets, community events, and neighborhood walks are the distribution channel. The palm card QR code bridges to the website.

### Digital ads
Best for name recognition at scale. Not effective for persuasion (research shows no detectable effect on changing voter preferences) but highly effective in a low-information race where the main problem is that nobody knows who's running. A voter who has seen the name three times on Instagram is more likely to rank him than skip him.

### Earned media
OPB, Willamette Week, Portland Tribune, Street Roots all covered the last District 2 race. Candidate forums, editorial board endorsements, and press coverage are free reach to engaged voters.

### Community networks
Union member communications, mosque and church networks, neighborhood association meetings, community events. High-trust, low-cost, slower.

## Digital ad strategy

### Platform selection
- Instagram and Facebook only, run through Meta's single ad manager
- Instagram reaches 25-45 demographic (active voting age, strong in District 2)
- Facebook reaches 45+ (reliable midterm voters, higher turnout rates)
- Skip Twitter/X entirely: smaller audience, worse geographic targeting, engagement skews toward national content not local races
- Let Meta's algorithm allocate budget between Instagram and Facebook based on performance

### Ad format: interactive polls
Rather than traditional campaign ads, run poll-format ads that engage voters while delivering the message. Example concepts:

**Poll 1 (sustainability angle):**
"Nabil for District 2. Do you prefer:"
- Digital campaign ads
- Mail ads
- I prefer no ads but I recognize it's necessary for new candidates to reach voters
Third option links to a page about the campaign's sustainability approach.

**Poll 2 (homelessness/accountability angle):**
"Should the county track whether homeless services actually work?"
- Yes
- No
- Wait, they don't already?
Third option links to the homelessness issue page.

**Poll 3 (general awareness):**
"Do you know who your county commissioner is?"
- Yes
- No
- What's a county commissioner?
Third option links to the county commissioner explainer page.

### Why polls work
- Instagram Stories poll stickers are a native feature that feels organic, not like an ad
- Engagement ads get higher reach per dollar because the algorithm rewards interaction
- The third option in each poll is the real move: it's the most interesting answer, it drives traffic to the site, and it positions Nabil as different from traditional campaigns
- Voters who engage with a poll ad are more likely to remember the name on the ballot

### Budget and timing
- Start at $500/week in August for testing
- Run three poll variations for a week each, see which gets the most engagement
- Put remaining budget behind the winner
- Scale up in September-October as ballots approach
- $5,000-10,000 total digital spend can reach 20,000-40,000 District 2 voters multiple times
- Start early: August-September rates are 30-50% lower than October rates

### Disclosure requirements
- Meta requires verification for political ads
- Multnomah County rules: if total communication spending exceeds $366, must prominently disclose funding sources
- All digital ads must comply with county disclosure rules (true original sources of contributions)
